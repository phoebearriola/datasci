Graphical Representations

The following are the data visualizations demonstrating species counts against elevations, habitat groupings, and overall frequencies Act03_Script.R].

Species Abundance vs Elevation Gradient

scatter_data <- data.frame( 
  SPPTotal = clean_data$SPPTotal,
  Elev = clean_data$Elev,
  Htypes = clean_data$Htypes
)

plot(scatter_data$Elev, scatter_data$SPPTotal, 
     xlab = "Elevation (m)", 
     ylab = "Species Total Count (SPPTotal)",
     main = "Species Total Count across Elevation Gradient", 
     col = factor(scatter_data$Htypes),
     pch = 19)

Observations per Habitat Type

split_habitat_totals <- split(clean_data$SPPTotal, clean_data$Htypes)
total_per_habitat <- sapply(split_habitat_totals, sum)

barplot(total_per_habitat, 
        xlab = "Habitat Types",

Written Interpretation

The survey area of Palawan displayed a lot of variation because of the different sea-level regions and the mountainous areas Act03_Script.R]. Elevation measurements ranged from 60 meters to 1,020 meters above sea level Act03_Script.R]. The data shows a clear pattern between common and uncommon species: Phlo is very abundant, while Babu is very rare Act03_Script.R]. Additionally, Agricultural/Secondary Growth (ASG) and Old Growth Forest (OGF) have the highest number of species, while cultivated land (CVT) could not support biodiversity. Human activities have disturbed the environment, harming important microhabitats Act03_Script.R].
